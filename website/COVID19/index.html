Introduction

See how your community is moving around differently due to COVID-19

As global communities respond to COVID-19, we've heard from public health officials that the same type of aggregated, anonymized insights we use in products such as Google Maps could be helpful as they make critical decisions to combat COVID-19.

These Community Mobility Reports aim to provide insights into what has changed in response to policies aimed at combating COVID-19. The reports chart movement trends over time by geography, across different categories of places such as retail and recreation, groceries and pharmacies, parks, transit stations, workplaces, and residential.

About this data: We sourced our mobility data from Google’s Community Mobility Reports. The data shows aggregated and anonymized summary statistics on visits to places -- such as grocery stores, transit stations, and parks -- are changing across the world. These categories are useful to understand how social distancing efforts and access to essential services are being implemented at a county level across the United States. Google obtains and calculates these community mobility reports from users who have opted-in to Location History for their Google Account, so the data represents a sample of our users. As with all samples, this may or may not represent the exact behavior of a wider population. The mobility data is available from February 15 to June 7 2020. In order to draw actionable insight from mobility trends, we also sourced confirmed COVID cases and deaths data from the Center of Disease Control and Prevention data supplier (https://usafacts.org/data/). This dataset provides the sum of cases by state from where the number of new cases per day are calculated. This data set provides the sum of deaths because of this virus as well. The data that corresponds to COVID19 is normalized by population estimated by the end of 2019 from the official data source at www.census.gov As Google’s location accuracy and data frequency varies widely across different geographic regions, we encountered many nulls as Google does not report mobility data if the region does not have sufficient statistically significant levels of data. We therefore removed nulls and applied linear interpolation to construct a statistically similar dataset to help bridge the gap in missing data.

Observation & Insights

The United States was slow to realize and react to COVID19’s deadly speed at breaching international borders: The world officially learned of COVID-19’s outbreak in China in January 2020. By January 23, Wuhan, a busy industrial hub of more than 11 million residents, were ordered to quarantine in place. By January 30th, the World Health Organization declared a global health emergency and the U.S. State Department warned travelers to avoid travelling to China. The following visualization shows the mobility trend in response to the growing spread of COVID cases in the United States from early February to March 2020. Click on the navigation bar at the top to see the escalating response taken by the W.H.O and the Trump Administration as the number of COVID cases multiples tenfold in a matter of 45 days. Hover over each state to see the average change in mobility and the number of COVID cases by state during each time period.



Though coastal states were faster than the rest of the country to enact “Shelter in Place” ordinances than the rest of the United States, the results were fixe: Coastal states such as California, New York, and Washington, due to their proximity to Asia and Europe, were the first places to report confirmed COVID cases and deaths in early 2020. Despite fast action from state and local governments, the number of cases and deaths spiked in New York as opposed to Washington and California. One hypothesis for why west coast states were able to “flatten the curve” in the first wave of COVID infection is due to the Trump administration’s early travel advisory and lockdown to and from Asia in January. However, as Europe also sustained a heavy wave of COVID infections, the Trump administration did not enact travel advisory to Europe until March. This policy unfortunately caused a heavy casualty in New York as COVID cases reached a high of 8,000 in a day.

Use the State and Date selector at the top right to see how mobility changes across different categories of places in each State. Compare California versus New York, and see how the number of cases different during the same time period.

United States Mobility versus Covid Cases and Deaths: Among the measures taken by the authorities to constrain the propagation of the virus are mobility restriction of the population or shelter in place. To understand the effect of mobility on the spread and effect of the virus we visualize the mobility on the number of cases recorded. This visualization consists of a bubble plot with the average mobility during the period of the study and the number of new cases per day normalized by population in each state. The horizontal axis represents the new cases per day and the vertical ages the average mobility. Additionally, the number of deaths because of the virus per day normalized by the state's population is represented by the size of the circles. Each bubble represents a different state. The details of the values are obtained by locating the mouse over each double. Also, each state on the legend (right side) can be highlighted to make easier its location in the plot. The insides of the visualization are the understanding of the effect of the mobility on the spread of the virus. Also, the plot illustrates the relative mortality rate of Covid19 on each state.



United States New Cases per Day: The new cases per day normalized by population are visualized by date to compare the spread of the virus in each state. Here, we have an idea when the cases started to increase and whether the spread was constrained or not at each state.
